Lesson 1: Introduction to 21st-Century Skills

Welcome! This lesson explores the essential skills you'll need not just for a future career, but to thrive in our complex, fast-paced, and interconnected world.

What are 21st-Century Skills?

The term “21st-century skills” refers to a broad set of knowledge, habits, and character traits that educators and employers see as vital for success in today's world.

The Six Core Skills

  • Critical Thinking: Generating questions, evaluating information, identifying patterns, and solving problems.
  • Communication: Sharing meaning and building understanding across different audiences and situations.
  • Collaboration: Working effectively in a team to achieve a shared goal.
  • Creativity: Generating new ideas and designing innovative solutions.
  • Citizenship: Acting respectfully and addressing issues at the local, national, or global level.
  • Growth Mindset: Persevering through challenges and believing in personal improvement through effort.

Collaborative Exercise 1: Survival on a Deserted Island

Scenario: Your team has crash-landed on a deserted island. You have managed to salvage 10 items from the plane wreckage. Your first task is to survive until rescue arrives.

  1. Form Teams: Get into groups of 4-5 students.
  2. Review the Items: Your instructor will provide the list.
  3. Prioritize: Rank the 10 items from most to least important for survival in the first 72 hours.
  4. Create a Plan: Use your top 3 items to secure water, shelter, and a rescue signal.
  5. Present Your Plan: Present your top 3 items and strategy in 3 minutes. Be ready to defend your choices.

The Three Categories of Skills

1. Learning Skills (The 4 C's)

  • Critical Thinking: Thinking outside the box.
  • Creativity: Seeing problems from different angles.
  • Collaboration: Compromising and working in teams.
  • Communication: Building lasting relationships.

2. Digital & Information Literacy Skills

  • Information Literacy: Finding, evaluating, and using information effectively.
  • Media Literacy: Analyzing and interacting with online messages.
  • Technology Literacy: Understanding how digital devices work.

Misinformation vs. Disinformation

  • Misinformation: False information spread by mistake.
  • Disinformation: False information spread intentionally to deceive.

3. Life Skills

  • Flexibility: Adapting to changes and new situations.
  • Leadership: Guiding a team to reach its goals.
  • Social Skills: Building networks through empathy, listening, and communication.

What Is Media Literacy?

Media literacy is the ability to analyze, evaluate, and create media messages. It helps you filter information and identify what is credible versus harmful or false.

Key Points in Media Literacy

  • Misinformation: False information spread accidentally.
  • Disinformation: False information spread intentionally.
  • Hate Speech: Attacks people based on race, religion, gender, etc. Media literacy helps reduce its spread.

Common Forms of Disinformation

  1. Satire or Parody: Humorous content that can be misinterpreted.
  2. Misleading Connection: Headlines that don’t match content.
  3. Misleading Content: Cropped or out-of-context information.
  4. False Context: Real content shared with a false narrative.
  5. Imposter Content: Fake use of a real logo or name.
  6. Manipulated Content: Edited or altered images and data.
  7. Fabricated Content: Entirely false content made to deceive.

How to Protect Yourself

  • Check the context — read the full story.
  • Verify the author’s credibility.
  • Consider the platform’s reliability.
  • Use fact-checking tools:
    • www.factcheck.org
    • www.snopes.com
    • www.mediabiasfactcheck.com
    • www.fotoforensics.com
    • Google Reverse Image Search
    • SearchSystems Public Records

Collaborative Exercise 2: Disinformation Detectives

Scenario: Your instructor will show 3-5 pieces of online content (news headline, post, meme, or video).

  1. Stay in your teams.
  2. Analyze the content. Decide if it's:
    • Reliable News
    • Misinformation
    • Disinformation
    • Satire or Parody
    • Misleading Content
  3. State your case: Provide two reasons to support your verdict (consider source, language, visuals, context).
  4. The Reveal: Instructor reveals the correct answer and discusses your findings.
