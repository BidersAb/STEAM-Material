Learning objectives

  • Define the six core 21st-century skills and give examples.
  • Differentiate misinformation and disinformation; spot common patterns.

Estimated time: 45–60 minutes · Prerequisites: none

Introduction to 21st-Century Skills

Welcome! This lesson explores the essential skills you’ll need not just for a future career, but to thrive in our complex, fast-paced, and interconnected world. These are the tools that will help you solve problems, create new things, and work effectively with others.

The term “21st-century skills” refers to a broad set of knowledge, habits, and character traits that educators and employers see as vital for success in today’s world. These skills go beyond traditional academic knowledge and are critical for navigating a world shaped by globalization, rapid technological change, and immense social diversity.

The Six Core Skills

  • Critical Thinking: Being a thoughtful detective—asking questions, evaluating information, identifying patterns, and using reasoning to solve problems.
  • Communication: Effectively interacting with others to share meaning and build understanding across situations and audiences.
  • Collaboration: Working effectively in a team, learning from others, and contributing toward shared goals.
  • Creativity: Generating new ideas and approaches to design innovative solutions and express unique perspectives.
  • Citizenship: Making a positive community impact by acting respectfully and engaging in local, national, or global issues.
  • Growth Mindset: Persevering through challenges with resilience, self-advocacy, and a belief in improvement through effort.

Collaborative Exercise 1: Survival on a Deserted Island

This exercise tests critical thinking, collaboration, and communication skills.

  1. Form teams of 4–5 students.
  2. Review the list of 10 salvaged items (provided by the instructor).
  3. Rank the items from most to least important for the first 72 hours of survival.
  4. Create a survival plan using your top 3 items for water, shelter, and signaling rescuers.
  5. Present your plan to the class in 3 minutes, defending your choices.

The Three Categories of Skills

1. Learning Skills (The 4 C's)

  • Creativity

2. Digital & Information Literacy Skills

  • Information Literacy: Finding, evaluating, and using information effectively.
  • Media Literacy: Analyzing, creating, and interacting with online content—spotting misinformation and disinformation.
  • Technology Literacy: Understanding and using technology effectively.

3. Life Skills

  • Flexibility

What Is Media Literacy?

Media literacy is the ability to analyze, evaluate, and create media messages, helping you identify credible vs. false or harmful content.

Misinformation vs. Disinformation

  • Misinformation: False information spread unintentionally.
  • Disinformation: False information spread intentionally to deceive.

Common Forms of Disinformation

  1. Satire or Parody
  2. Misleading Connection
  3. Misleading Content
  4. False Context
  5. Imposter Content
  6. Manipulated Content
  7. Fabricated Content

How to Protect Yourself

  • Check the full context, not just headlines.
  • Verify the author’s reliability.
  • Consider the platform’s influence on content.
  • Use fact-checking tools:
    • factcheck.org
    • snopes.com
    • mediabiasfactcheck.com
    • FotoForensics
    • Google Reverse Image Search
    • International Public Records

Collaborative Exercise 2: Disinformation Detectives

  1. Stay in your teams.
  2. Analyze each piece of content and classify it as reliable news, misinformation, disinformation, satire, or misleading content.
  3. Provide at least two reasons for your verdict.
  4. Compare findings in a class discussion.
