Introduction to Arduino
Welcome to the world of physical computing! This lesson introduces you to Arduino, an open-source platform that makes building interactive electronics projects accessible to everyone, from artists and designers to students and hobbyists. With an Arduino, you can create projects that sense the environment and react to it, from simple blinking lights to complex robots.

What is Arduino?
Arduino is a complete platform consisting of two main parts:

  1. A physical programmable circuit board, often referred to as a microcontroller.
  2. A piece of software, or IDE (Integrated Development Environment), that runs on your computer. You use the IDE to write and upload code to the physical board.
Before we dive into the Arduino board itself, it’s important to understand what a microcontroller is. A microcontroller is a compact integrated circuit (IC) device designed to control other parts of an electronic system. Think of it as a mini-computer on a single chip, optimized for interacting with components like sensors, motors, and lights.

The Arduino UNO Board: A Detailed Look

The Arduino UNO is the most popular and standard board, highly recommended for beginners because it is easy to use. It includes everything you need to get started. Let’s break down its key parts, as seen in the diagram:

  • 1 & 2 - Power (USB / Barrel Jack): Every Arduino needs power. The UNO can be powered either from a USB cable connected to your computer (1) or a wall power supply connected to the barrel jack (2). The USB connection is also how you will load your code onto the board.
  • 3-9 - Pins: The pins are where you connect wires to build your circuits. They have different functions:
    • GND (3): 'Ground'. Several GND pins can be used to ground your circuit.
    • 5V (4) & 3.3V (5): Supply 5 volts and 3.3 volts of power, respectively.
    • Analog In (6): Pins A0 through A5 can read signals from analog sensors (like temperature sensors) and convert them into digital values.
    • Digital (7): Pins 0 through 13 can be used for digital input (e.g., reading button pushes) and digital output (e.g., powering an LED).
    • PWM (8): Pins with a tilde ~ (3, 5, 6, 9, 10, 11) can simulate analog output using Pulse-Width Modulation (PWM), useful for effects like fading LEDs.
    • AREF (9): Analog Reference pin for advanced uses (usually left alone).
  • 10 - Reset Button: Restarts the loaded code on the Arduino when pressed.
  • 11 - Power LED Indicator: Lights up when the Arduino is powered.
  • 12 - TX RX LEDs: Flash when the Arduino transmits or receives data, such as during program upload.
  • 13 - Main IC: The large black chip that acts as the brain of the Arduino.
  • 14 - Voltage Regulator: Controls the voltage to protect the Arduino from overvoltage; never exceed 20 volts.

Essential Tools for Prototyping

When working with an Arduino, you’ll use a few key components to build your circuits without soldering:

  • Breadboard: A base for building temporary circuits, with rows of holes that are electrically connected internally.
    • Terminal Strips: Main area to place components; holes in each horizontal row (e.g., row 1, columns a-e) are connected.
    • Power Rails: Vertical strips on the sides marked + and – to supply power and ground.
  • Jumper Wires: Wires with solid tips for connecting components on the breadboard and to Arduino pins.
  • Resistors: Components that resist current flow, used to protect sensitive parts like LEDs. Measured in ohms (Ω).
  • LED (Light-Emitting Diode): Emits light when current flows. Polarized, with a long positive leg (anode) and short negative leg (cathode).

Arduino Exercises

Beginner Exercise: Your First Circuit - Blinking an LED

This is the "Hello, World!" of electronics. Build a simple circuit and write code to make an LED blink on and off.

  1. Build the Circuit:
    • Connect Arduino’s GND pin to the negative (blue) power rail on your breadboard with a jumper wire.
    • Place an LED on the breadboard.
    • Connect the LED’s short leg (cathode) to the negative rail on the breadboard.
    • Connect one leg of a 220Ω resistor to the same row as the LED’s long leg (anode).
    • Connect the other leg of the resistor to a different breadboard row.
    • Use a jumper wire to connect that row to Digital Pin 13 on the Arduino.
  2. Write the Code: Use this code in the Arduino IDE: 
    // This code will make an LED blink on and off
void setup() {
  pinMode(13, OUTPUT);
}

void loop() {
  digitalWrite(13, HIGH);  // Turn LED on
  delay(1000);             // Wait 1 second
  digitalWrite(13, LOW);   // Turn LED off
  delay(1000);             // Wait 1 second
}
  3. Upload and Run: Connect Arduino to your computer via USB, select the correct board and port, then upload. Your LED should blink!

Challenge Exercise: Traffic Light Controller

Control multiple LEDs in sequence simulating a traffic light.

  1. Build the Circuit: You’ll need three LEDs: red, yellow, green.
    • Connect each LED to its own digital pin (e.g., red to pin 11, yellow to pin 12, green to pin 13).
    • Each LED needs its own 220Ω resistor connected to its long leg (anode).
    • Connect all LED short legs (cathode) to the ground rail on the breadboard.
  2. Write the Code: Write a loop() function for this sequence:
    • Green ON for 5 seconds.
    • Green OFF, Yellow ON for 2 seconds.
    • Yellow OFF, Red ON for 5 seconds.
    • Repeat endlessly.
  3. Hint: Use multiple digitalWrite() and delay() commands to control LEDs in the correct order.
